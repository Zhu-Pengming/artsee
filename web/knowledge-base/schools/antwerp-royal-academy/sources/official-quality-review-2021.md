# Quality Enhancement Review of Bachelor and Master in the Visual Arts at Royal Academy of Fine Arts Antwerp

## TL;DR
The Royal Academy of Fine Arts Antwerp (RAFA) offers a comprehensive Bachelor and Master in Visual Arts program with a strong emphasis on studio practice, technical skills, and interdisciplinary collaboration. The program is designed to foster a diverse and inclusive learning environment, with a focus on sustainability and community engagement. The review highlights RAFA's commitment to quality assurance and continuous improvement, noting areas for potential enhancement, particularly in terms of internationalization and interdisciplinary research.

## Key Claims

### Introduction
- RAFA is one of the oldest art schools in the world, established in 1663, and is part of the Artesis-Plantijn University College in Flanders, Belgium.
- The Bachelor and Master in Visual Arts programs are the core of RAFA's educational offer, complemented by an Educational Master's program and a PhD program in collaboration with the University of Antwerp and Antwerp Research Institute of the Arts (ARIA).
- The review was conducted by an international team from EQ-Arts, with support from MusiQuE, following a three-stage process including a Self-evaluation Report (SER), virtual site visit, and final report.

### Program Goals and Context
- RAFA's mission is to be an international biotope, focusing on the studio as a creative space and emphasizing the synergy between education, research, and community service.
- The Academy has established strategic and operational goals linked to the AP University College's strategic plan, focusing on active citizenship, learning benefits, unique educational offerings, and innovation.
- The ADAPT project aims to improve performance and efficiency, reconfigure management and support services, and transform the main campus site, potentially creating new interdisciplinary and research spaces.
- RAFA's objectives include diversity, sustainability, and Do It Yourself/Do It Together (DIY/DIT) approaches, with a focus on embedding diverse perspectives and inclusive ways of working within the curriculum.
- The Academy plans to maintain its student population at around 600, with a focus on reducing sociocultural barriers for incoming students to create a more diverse student body.

### Educational Processes
- The Visual Arts Program comprises a 180-credit Bachelor degree and a 60-credit Master degree, available in six specialisms: Photography, Graphic Design, Jewellery Design, Theatre Costume, Fashion, and Fine Arts.
- The Fine Arts specialism includes Sculpture, Painting, In Situ, and Printmaking options, offered in both Dutch and English at both Bachelor and Master levels.
- The program attracts a substantial number of international students (60% in 2019-20), with Fashion being the largest specialism.
- The Flemish higher education system follows the Bologna agreement, with the 2003 Structural Decree establishing clusters of universities and university colleges and a public accreditation system.
- The review team found that the program's goals are clearly articulated within the Academy's mission and vision, with a growing awareness of established strategic goals among staff.
- The Roadmap outlines clear plans for the involvement of key stakeholders in the establishment, monitoring, and review of objectives for the development of the program.
- The Academy's mission and vision take account of the global context, but the program could benefit from expressing the theme of globalization more clearly within the curriculum.
- The Roadmap suggests that global perspectives will be brought into the program through its international student body, but it is recommended that the strategic target related to internationalization be expanded to include a greater range of non-European reference points within the program content.
- Objectives related to the inclusivity of the program are outlined within the SER and the Roadmap, but very little is currently collected on applicant and student profiles that would enable the Academy to track its progress in these areas.

### Student Profiles
- The program has around 568 students each year, evenly split between the Dutch language and English language pathways.
- Fashion is the largest specialism, with around 200 students, followed by Graphic Design with around 100 students.
- The current structure of the higher education system in Flanders stems from the region's implementation of the Bologna agreement in 2003, including the adoption of first and second cycle programs (Bachelor and Master) and the European Credit Transfer System (ECTS).
- The review team recommends that, with support from AP University College, data on applicant profiles is systematically collected, in addition to data on student progress and employment outcomes, in order to enable analysis of access to and attainment within the program throughout the study cycle, for all students including those from underrepresented socio-cultural backgrounds.

### Teaching Staff
- There are around 112 teachers serving the programs, with 60.9 FTE.
- The Academy purposefully appoints teachers with strong research profiles and researchers who can contribute to studio teaching.
- A number of funded short-term research projects and doctoral fellowships are available on an annual basis as research opportunities for staff, alumni, and external applicants, creating a strong research culture within the institution.
- Students have the opportunity to interact with researchers in the studio, through extra-curricular activities such as the Extra Academy and masterclasses guided by PhD students and researchers.
- Four interdisciplinary research-groups enable the development of specific areas of expertise and reinforce the development of students as artist-researchers.
- The Academy has also recently formalized its public lectures into the Guess who's coming to dinner series, which brings interdisciplinary artists into the institution to talk about their practice.
- A new initiative, the Expanding Academy, seeks to build on these activities and in collaboration with a group of other European arts organisations, will link artistic research with social and environmental issues.

### Facilities, Resources, and Support
- The Academy has three dedicated exhibition spaces at Campus Mutsaard, including the Long Gallery, the Winter Garden, and the Temple.
- Students are able to submit proposals for independently organized exhibitions to the Planning Committee and the annual Starter Prize organized by the Student Council provides the opportunity for one student each year to organize a funded project within one of the exhibition spaces.
- Students also benefit from a number of opportunities to present their work externally, such as the Schmuck fair (jewellery), FoMU (photography), and the Het Bos art centre (costume design).
- Selected student work is also published by the Academy (for example through the Track Report) and externally, in art magazines such as Metropolis M and through partnerships with publishers such as Stockmans and Boek.be.
- The Academy is bound by Structural Decree to deliver research-based arts education and to embed research competencies in both the Bachelor and Master programs as distinct learning outcomes.
- The focus on research within the curriculum grows as students progress from the first Bachelor year through to the Master year, when the final Master project incorporates documentation of the student's research.
- Master students also have the option of writing a thesis. The biennial Drawing Marathon offers another opportunity for intensive artistic research, wherein students can experiment with various contexts, subjects, and techniques, alongside lecturers, alumni, and overseas partner institutions (via live stream) over a 24-hour period.
- In spite of these activities and interventions, there is still a tendency for students to understand research as theoretical and text-based rather than something embedded within or enacted through their practice.

### Assessment
- The Visual Arts Programme employs a range of formative and summative assessment methods, including practical assignments, presentations, written and oral examinations, and written assignments.
- Assessment and teaching methods are aligned in the sense that more theoretical, lecture-based courses generally have written examinations, whereas studio practice is assessed through the presentation of practical work.
- The Academy has identified the need to devise a matrix that links learning objectives, curriculum content, and assessment methods.
- Studio work is most commonly assessed through permanent evaluation, a final assessment at the end of the year with either an internal jury or for the Master program, an external jury.
- These assessment methods support program learning outcomes related to the development of artistic skills and individual visual language or artistic imagery, alongside the ability to communicate this to a critical audience.
- The external element of the Master permanent evaluation, alongside the assessment of the optional Master's thesis by written assignment and oral presentation, enables students to demonstrate the higher-level learning outcomes related to independent artistic research and the ability to take and defend a critical position when situating their work within a broader context.
- For final evaluations, a 20-point marking system is used, with 10 points required to pass. Pass/fail grades are also being trialled in some cases.
- Interim assessments use a variety of systems across studios, with some contributing to the summative assessment outcome and others not.
- The Academy reviews its assessment methods at the end of each cycle through discussion with teaching staff about issues and good practice.
- Student feedback is sought through cyclical course-level surveys and annual student focus groups.
- There is a trial and error approach to the development of assessment methods within the program, whereby the short lines of communication within the institution allow the Heads of Programme to understand what is and is not working.
- The assessment process guards against individual subjective evaluation through the use of the four-eye principle, or assessment by several individuals at once, both in the final jury evaluations and in other modes of assessment throughout the year.
- The introduction of externality to jury assessments through the use of artists, alumni, critics, gallerists, and curators as jury members is used both as a means of mitigating against internal bias in the assessment process and as a means of external evaluation of the program as a whole.
- Some external juries have the opportunity of assessing students twice, in January and June, which helps to give jury members an insight into students' creative process and individual artistic development.
- Juries are expected to reach a consensus on marking, however there are currently no established institutional protocols for their operation, therefore the process for agreeing marks varies between studios.
- All juries include a Chair and a Secretary who have the role of refocusing the discussion on learning outcomes when required.

## Evidence/Methodology
The review was conducted by an international team from EQ-Arts, with support from MusiQuE, following a three-stage process including a Self-evaluation Report (SER), virtual site visit, and final report. The review team analyzed the SER and supporting documents, conducted virtual tours of the Faculty buildings, workshops, studios, and exhibition spaces, and held meetings with the Dean of the Academy, Heads of Programmes, the Quality and Education Development Advisor, students, alumni, teachers, Studio Coordinators, staff with responsibility for research, staff in administration and learning support roles, and representatives of the work field.

## Notes/Analysis

- The review highlights RAFA's commitment to quality assurance and continuous improvement, noting areas for potential enhancement, particularly in terms of internationalization and interdisciplinary research.
- The program's focus on studio practice and technical skills is a strength, but there is a need to further develop interdisciplinary research and global perspectives within the curriculum.
- The Academy's approach to internationalization is commendable, with a focus on the international diversity of the student body and a growing number of partnerships with institutions around the world.
- The review team recommends that the Academy finds effective mechanisms for drawing the global perspectives of its students into the design and content of the curriculum.
- The Academy's assessment methods are robust, with a focus on both formative and summative assessment, and a strong emphasis on feedback and student engagement.
- The review team recommends that the Academy develops a more explicit understanding of how grades are awarded and improves the transparency of marking practices.

## Additional Sections

### Student Life
- RAFA offers a range of student life activities, including student organizations, events, and opportunities for international travel and exchange.
- The Academy provides support for international students through a dedicated induction program and study buddy system.
- The Academy's website includes a comprehensive Quick Start Guide for International Students, providing practical information on traveling to and living in Antwerp and on facilities and services within the Academy.

### Career Outcomes
- RAFA has a strong track record of career outcomes for its graduates, with many going on to work in the arts and design industries.
- The Academy's focus on technical skills and interdisciplinary collaboration prepares students for a wide range of careers, including art direction, graphic design, fashion design, photography, and more.
- The Academy's partnerships with industry leaders and its strong research culture provide students with valuable opportunities for internships and networking.

### Conclusion
The Royal Academy of Fine Arts Antwerp offers a comprehensive and high-quality Bachelor and Master in Visual Arts program, with a strong emphasis on studio practice, technical skills, and interdisciplinary collaboration. The Academy's commitment to quality assurance and continuous improvement is evident in the review process and the resulting recommendations. While there are areas for potential enhancement, particularly in terms of internationalization and interdisciplinary research, the Academy's strong foundation and strategic vision position it well for continued success in the future.
